Dale Slaughter

I am a semiretired archaeologist and professional photographer. My primary photographic interest is Alaskan landscapes with and without manmade objected. I find myself equally attracted to pristine nature and ghostly appeal derelict buildings and other artifacts. I work in both color and black and white, although I much prefer the enhanced level of abstraction provided by black and white photography. Since 2000 all of my black and white photographs have been made with large format cameras using 4 x 5 in. sheet film. I find the versatility of large format cameras and the exquisite negative detail obtainable with them is more than adequate compensation for the bulky, heavy, and cumbersome nature of the equipment.

My images are computer generated from scanned negatives and printed using graphics software. Image manipulation is limited to tonal adjustments and improving image sharpness. The latter is necessary because the scanning process degrades image sharpness somewhat. I do not digitally remove elements from an image or create composite images using elements from more than one source. My feeling is that Alaskan landscapes are dramatic enough without artificial manipulation. Images are printed with an Epson Stylus Pro 3800 inkjet printer with Epson UltraChrome K3 inks on 100% cotton rag paper.

I have had the good fortune to travel widely in Alaska and this is reflected in the gallery. The various gallery names are self-explanatory geographically except for Potpourri which, as the name implies, is a miscellaneous collections of images that do not handily fit in the other categories. Additional information on the galleries and individual images will be forth coming. For the present, I shall only on comment on two images, both from the Aleutian Islands, to give the viewer an appreciation of the vastness of Alaska and some of the problems that can be encountered travelling there. The first image, (Kittiwakes in Fog, Buldir Island, 1989) was taken on tiny fog-shrouded Buldir Island the western Aleutians. Buldir Island is over 1,400 miles from Anchorage, about the same distance from Anchorage as is Seattle. The second image (Windspeed off Garaloi Island, 1989) demonstrates that travel in Alaska can be uncomfortable both physically and psychologically. This photograph was taken June 18, 1989 aboard the research vessel Tiglax off the north shore of Garaloi Island in the Central Aleutians during a frightful storm. The actual wind speed is unknown because the needle of the anemometer is restrained by the peg which is past 120 knots (138 mile per hour)
